# Data Preprocessing: Handling Outliers, Missing Values, and Skewed Data

---

## 1. 🧱 The Core Concept (Basics Refresh)

In production machine learning systems, raw data is messy, biased, and hostile to optimization algorithms. Preprocessing is not about "cleaning" data; it is about **transforming empirical distributions into representations that match the mathematical assumptions of your downstream estimators.**

```
                         [ RAW DATA PIPELINE ]
                                   │
         ┌─────────────────────────┼─────────────────────────┐
         ▼                         ▼                         ▼
   ┌───────────┐             ┌───────────┐             ┌───────────┐
   │ Outliers  │             │  Missing  │             │  Skewed   │
   └─────┬─────┘             └─────┬─────┘             └─────┬─────┘
         │                         │                         │
         ├─ IQR / Z-score          ├─ MCAR / MAR / MNAR      ├─ Log / Box-Cox
         └─ Robust Scaling         └─ Iterative Imputer      └─ Yeo-Johnson
```

### 1.1 Outliers: Definition & Mathematical Detection
An outlier is an observation that deviates so significantly from other observations as to arouse suspicion that it was generated by a different mechanism.

#### Univariate Detection
*   **Z-Score (Parametric - Assumes Normality):**
    $$Z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$$
    Typically, $|Z| > 3$ flags an outlier (covering $99.73\%$ of a normal distribution).
*   **Interquartile Range (IQR) (Non-Parametric):**
    $$\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1$$
    Boundaries are defined as:
    $$\text{Lower Bound} = Q_1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$$
    $$\text{Upper Bound} = Q_3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$$
    *Note: The $1.5$ multiplier corresponds to approximately $\pm 2.698\sigma$ in a standard normal distribution.*

#### Multivariate Detection
*   **Mahalanobis Distance:** Measures how many standard deviations away $x$ is from the mean of a multivariate distribution, accounting for covariance:
    $$D_M(x) = \sqrt{(x - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (x - \mu)}$$
    where $\Sigma$ is the covariance matrix. This detects structural anomalies that univariate methods miss (e.g., an individual who is $7\text{ft}$ tall but weighs only $100\text{lbs}$).

### 1.2 Missing Values: The Taxonomy of Loss
How data goes missing dictates how you must resolve it. Simply dropping rows or imputing averages without analyzing the missingness mechanism introduces severe selection bias.

| Type | Definition | Mathematical Expression | Example | Mitigations |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **MCAR** (Missing Completely at Random) | Missingness is entirely independent of both observed and unobserved data. | $P(M \mid Y_{obs}, Y_{mis}) = P(M)$ | Equipment failure due to random voltage spikes. | Listwise deletion (safe but inefficient), mean/median imputation. |
| **MAR** (Missing at Random) | Missingness depends systematically on observed data, but not on the missing value itself. | $P(M \mid Y_{obs}, Y_{mis}) = P(M \mid Y_{obs})$ | Younger survey respondents are less likely to report their income, but income is missing at random *within* age brackets. | Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE), KNN imputation, predictor-based modeling. |
| **MNAR** (Missing Not at Random) | Missingness depends directly on the unobserved missing value. | $P(M \mid Y_{obs}, Y_{mis}) \neq P(M \mid Y_{obs})$ | High-income earners refuse to disclose income due to privacy concerns. | Add missingness indicator features ($I_{missing}$), Heckman selection models, joint distribution modeling. |

### 1.3 Skewed Data: Asymmetry in Feature Space
Skewness is the measure of the asymmetry of a probability distribution about its mean.

$$\gamma_1 = E\left[\left(\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^3\right]$$

```
     Right (Positive) Skew                   Left (Negative) Skew
         Mode                                         Mode
        / \                                           / \
       /   \   Median                                /   \
      /     \   \                                   /     \
     /       \   Mean                              /       \
   _/_________\___\_______                       _/_________\_______
```

*   **Right (Positive) Skew ($\gamma_1 > 0$):** The right tail is long/fat; the mean is typically greater than the median. Common in physical measurements, incomes, and counting metrics (e.g., page views).
*   **Left (Negative) Skew ($\gamma_1 < 0$):** The left tail is long/fat; the mean is typically less than the median. Common in age-of-death demographics or test scores with a ceiling effect.

## 2. ⚙️ Under the Hood (Internal Mechanics & Architecture)

As a Staff Engineer, you must look past simple APIs like `scikit-learn` and understand how these transformations alter loss landscapes, affect optimization trajectories, and scale across distributed datasets.

### 2.1 Impact on Optimization & Loss Landscapes
Why do linear models and neural networks struggle with outliers and skewed distributions?

#### Gradient Volatility
Consider a linear regression model optimized via Mean Squared Error (MSE):

$$L(w) = \frac{1}{2N}\sum_{i=1}^N (y_i - w^T x_i)^2$$

The gradient with respect to the weights $w$ is:

$$\nabla_w L = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N (y_i - w^T x_i) x_i$$

If a single outlier point $x_{out}$ has a feature value or target value $100\times$ larger than the mean, the corresponding gradient step is scaled by the squared magnitude of that outlier. 

This causes the gradient update to be dominated by the outlier, leading to **gradient explosion**, severe oscillations, and destabilization of SGD.

```
       Unscaled / Skewed Features               Scaled / Normalized Features
       
             w2                                       w2
             │                                        │
        ───┐ │ ┌───                                ┌──┴──┐
       /   │ │ │   \                             /   │   \
      │ ( )│ │ │( ) │                           │   ( )   │
      │  \─┼─┼─┼─/  │                           │    │    │
       \   │ │ │   /                             \   │   /
        ───┘ │ └───                               └──┬──┘
             └────────── w1                          └────────── w1
         (Oscillatory Steps)                      (Direct Descent)
```

#### Hessian Conditioning
Skewed features yield an ill-conditioned Hessian matrix $H = \nabla^2 L(w) = \frac{1}{N}X^T X$. 

The ratio of the maximum to minimum eigenvalues of the Hessian ($\kappa = \lambda_{max}/\lambda_{min}$, known as the **condition number**) becomes extremely high. 

A high condition number turns the loss contour into a steep, narrow canyon. Gradient descent bounces back and forth across the canyon walls rather than moving down the valley toward the global minimum. 

Scaling and de-skewing features squeezes $\kappa$ toward 1, making the loss contours isotropic and speeding up convergence.

### 2.2 Mathematical Transformations: Under the Hood

#### Log Transformation
$$y = \log(x + 1)$$
Used for highly right-skewed positive data. It scales down extreme values by mapping multiplicative relationships to additive ones. 

However, it cannot handle negative values and compresses the variance of large values so much that valuable signals in the tail can be lost.

#### Box-Cox Transformation
A parametric power transformation that stabilizes variance and normalizes distributions. It requires all input values to be strictly positive ($x > 0$):

$$x^{(\lambda)} = \begin{cases} 
\frac{x^\lambda - 1}{\lambda} & \text{if } \lambda \neq 0 \\ 
\log(x) & \text{if } \lambda = 0 
\end{cases}$$

The optimal parameter $\lambda$ is estimated via Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE):

$$\mathcal{L}(\lambda) = -\frac{n}{2} \log(\hat{\sigma}^2(\lambda)) + (\lambda - 1) \sum_{i=1}^n \log(x_i)$$

where $\hat{\sigma}^2(\lambda)$ is the variance of the transformed data.

#### Yeo-Johnson Transformation
An extension of Box-Cox that allows for zero and negative values:

$$\psi(\lambda, x) = \begin{cases}
\frac{(x + 1)^\lambda - 1}{\lambda} & \text{if } \lambda \neq 0, x \ge 0 \\
\log(x + 1) & \text{if } \lambda = 0, x \ge 0 \\
-\frac{(-x + 1)^{2 - \lambda} - 1}{2 - \lambda} & \text{if } \lambda \neq 2, x < 0 \\
-\log(-x + 1) & \text{if } \lambda = 2, x < 0
\end{cases}$$

#### Robust Scaling vs. Standard Scaling
*   **Standard Scaler ($z$-score):**
    $$x_{scaled} = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$$
    If outliers exist, $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are heavily skewed outward. Consequently, non-outlier data gets squashed into a tiny, narrow cluster, hiding the variance from the model.
*   **Robust Scaler:**
    $$x_{scaled} = \frac{x - \text{median}(x)}{\text{IQR}(x)}$$
    Because the median and IQR are highly robust to outliers (their breakdown point is up to 50% contamination), this scaler guarantees that the central bulk of the data is mapped to a standard scale, leaving outliers isolated in the periphery where they can be explicitly handled.

### 2.3 Algorithmic Sensitivities
Not all machine learning models require preprocessing.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                        ALGORITHMIC SENSITIVITY                         │
├───────────────────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Invariant                     │ Highly Sensitive                       │
├───────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • Tree Models (XGBoost, etc.) │ • Linear Models (Lasso, Ridge)         │
│ • Naive Bayes                 │ • Deep Learning (MLPs, CNNs)           │
│                               │ • Distance-based (KNN, K-Means, SVM)   │
└───────────────────────────────┴────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### Tree-Based Models (XGBoost, LightGBM, Random Forests)
*   **Outliers & Skew:** Trees are invariant to monotonic transformations. Because tree nodes split based on rank orders ($X_j \le t$), scaling, logging, or transforming features has zero impact on split selection.
*   **Missing Values:** Modern implementations like XGBoost handle missing values natively. During training, at each node split, XGBoost assigns all missing values to whichever default branch (left or right) minimizes the training loss:
    $$\mathcal{L}_{split} = \max \left( Gain(Missing \to Left), Gain(Missing \to Right) \right)$$
    In production, if a value is missing, it takes the default direction learned during training.

#### Distance-Based & Kernel-Based Models (KNN, K-Means, SVM)
*   These algorithms rely directly on the Euclidean distance:
    $$d(u,v) = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^d (u_i - v_i)^2}$$
    If feature $A$ has a range of $[0, 10^6]$ (highly skewed/unscaled) and feature $B$ has a range of $[0, 1]$, $d(u,v)$ is entirely dominated by feature $A$. This leaves feature $B$ with zero influence on the model, even if it has higher predictive power.

#### Deep Learning & Generalized Linear Models (GLMs)
*   **Linear Models:** Need normalized and non-skewed features to prevent optimization loops and avoid violating the assumption of homoscedasticity (constant variance of errors).
*   **Neural Networks:** Skewed inputs slow down learning due to standard activation function limits. For example, large values can push Sigmoid and Tanh activations into saturation regions, causing vanishing gradients.

### 2.4 Production Scaling & Distributed Computation
When scale reaches billions of rows (petabytes), running a naive `fit_transform` in-memory on a single machine is impossible.

#### Distributed Imputation & Scaling in Spark
To perform Z-score normalization or mean imputation in a distributed engine like Apache Spark, you must avoid shuffling the entire dataset.

1.  **Map Stage:** Each worker computes local partition-level statistics: $\sum x_i$, $\sum x_i^2$, and the local count $n$.
2.  **Reduce Stage:** Collect the local statistics to compute global parameters ($\mu$ and $\sigma$) using **Welford’s Algorithm** for running variance to avoid catastrophic numerical cancellation:
    $$M_1 = x_1, \quad M_k = M_{k-1} + \frac{x_k - M_{k-1}}{k}$$
    $$S_k = S_{k-1} + (x_k - M_{k-1})(x_k - M_k)$$
3.  **Broadcast Stage:** Broadcast the global $\mu$ and $\sigma$ parameters back to all worker nodes.
4.  **Map Stage (Transform):** Workers apply the transformation row-wise in parallel:
    $$x_i' = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sigma}$$
    This requires zero shuffle operations across the network, scaling linearly ($\mathcal{O}(N)$).

#### Distributed Quantiles (for IQR & Box-Cox)
Exact quantile computation requires sorting, which is highly expensive over a network ($\mathcal{O}(N \log N)$ shuffle). 

To solve this, production pipelines use approximation algorithms like the **Greenwald-Khanna (GK)** algorithm or **t-Digest** to build highly accurate quantile sketches on distributed partitions with a bounded error guarantee $\epsilon$.

### 3.1 Scenario 1: High-Throughput Ads Auction System (Extreme Skew & MNAR Missingness)

#### Interviewer Probe
> *"We are building an ad-click prediction engine. The feature `advertiser_historical_spend` is highly skewed (ranges from $0 to $100M, power-law distributed). Additionally, the feature `user_historical_bid_amount` is missing for 85% of users because they have never participated in a real-time auction (MNAR).*
>
> *How do you build a robust feature engineering pipeline to handle these issues for a Logistic Regression model? Walk me through the mathematical formulations, trade-offs, and data-leakage prevention strategy."*

```
                              [ INCOMING ADS DATA ]
                                        │
                 ┌──────────────────────┴──────────────────────┐
                 ▼                                             ▼
     [ advertiser_hist_spend ]                     [ user_hist_bid_amount ]
                 │                                             │
      Power-Law / Extreme Skew                            85% Missing
                 │                                             │
         Yeo-Johnson Trans.                            Indicator Feature:
     (Handles exact 0 values)                         I_missing ∈ {0, 1}
                 │                                             │
         Robust Scaling:                             Conditional Imputation
     (Median/IQR Standardization)                      (e.g., KNN/Iterative)
                 │                                             │
                 └──────────────────────┬──────────────────────┘
                                        ▼
                            [ CONCATENATED FEATURES ]
                                        │
                                 Logistic Reg.
```

#### Perfect Response
1.  **De-skewing the Power-Law (`advertiser_historical_spend`):**
    *   Since spend contains exact zero values (advertisers who have signed up but spent nothing), a standard Box-Cox transformation is mathematically invalid because it requires $x > 0$.
    *   I would apply the **Yeo-Johnson transformation** to stabilize variance and reshape the power-law distribution into an approximately normal distribution. 
    *   Following the Yeo-Johnson step, I will apply a **Robust Scaler** ($x_{scaled} = \frac{x - \text{median}}{\text{IQR}}$). Since ad spend contains massive outlier spenders, Standard Scaling (using mean and standard deviation) would compress the non-outlier advertisers into a narrow band, rendering the feature less effective for the model.

2.  **Addressing the MNAR Character of `user_historical_bid_amount`:**
    *   An 85% missing rate indicates that the missingness itself contains significant predictive signal (i.e., the user is inactive or new).
    *   I will first create a binary missingness indicator column:
        $$I_{missing} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } x \text{ is missing} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
    *   For the main feature column, instead of using mean imputation (which distorts variance and introduces massive bias), I will use **conditional imputation** based on other observed features (e.g., user country, age, and device type).
    *   If latency constraints in the production pipeline are tight, I will use a fast median-by-category imputation (e.g., median bid amount grouped by user country). If latency permits, a lightweight predictive model (like a fast regressor trained on the non-missing subset) can be evaluated.

3.  **Data Leakage Prevention:**
    *   *Critical Design Choice:* I will strictly split the data into training, validation, and test sets **before** any preprocessing.
    *   The parameters for the transformations (the optimal $\lambda$ for Yeo-Johnson, the median and IQR values, and the category-specific median values for imputation) must be learned **only** from the training set.
    *   These learned parameters will be persisted as metadata in our model registry. During validation, test, and production serving, the pipeline will load these static parameters to transform incoming features. This prevents information from the validation or test sets from leaking into the training step.

### 3.2 Scenario 2: Real-time Credit Card Fraud Detection (Univariate vs. Multivariate Outliers)

#### Interviewer Probe
> *"We have a real-time transaction processing stream ($SLA < 10\text{ms}$). An incoming transaction has two features: `distance_from_home` ($100\text{ miles}$) and `transaction_amount` ($150$). Separately, these values are well within normal univariate bounds ($Z$-score $< 1.5$ for both).*
>
> *However, combined, they represent a highly anomalous event (a large distance from home for a small, unusual transaction type).*
>
> *How do you architect a system to detect and handle this multivariate outlier under strict latency requirements? Explain the exact mathematical approaches you would use."*

```
                              [ TRANSACTION STREAM ] (SLA < 10ms)
                                       │
                    ┌──────────────────┴──────────────────┐
                    ▼                                     ▼
          [ Univariate Check ]                   [ Multivariate Check ]
          • dist: 100 miles (Z=1.2)              • Mahalanobis Distance: DM
          • amount: $150 (Z=0.8)                   Needs Σ^-1 (Covariance Matrix)
                    │                                     │
                    ▼                                     ▼
              [ OK Alone ]                         [ Latency Risk! ]
                    │                           ┌─────────┴─────────┐
                    │                           ▼                   ▼
                    │                     Offline Step:       Online Step:
                    │                     Compute Σ^-1        Evaluate Projection:
                    │                     and save vector     z = W^T x + b
                    │                           │                   │
                    └───────────────────────────┼───────────────────┘
                                                ▼
                                    [ ANOMALY SCORE ENGINE ]
```

#### Perfect Response
1.  **Why Univariate Methods Fail:**
    *   Univariate outlier detection methods like IQR or $Z$-score process features independently in isolation. They fail to capture correlation structures. 
    *   If a user frequently spends $150 near home, and occasionally travels $100 miles for large purchases, both numbers look completely typical on their own. The anomaly lies in the correlation space, which can only be detected via multivariate approaches.

2.  **Multivariate Mathematical Selection & Trade-Offs:**
    *   *Option A: Mahalanobis Distance.* This approach works well for capturing linear correlation structures:
        $$D_M(x) = \sqrt{(x - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (x - \mu)}$$
        For a 2-dimensional feature space, evaluating this formula is incredibly fast. The inversion of the covariance matrix $\Sigma^{-1}$ is calculated offline on historical data. During live inference, evaluating $D_M(x)$ requires only a few matrix multiplications (a vector-matrix-vector product), which easily runs in $<1\text{ms}$.
    *   *Option B: Isolation Forest (iForest).* Non-parametric option that isolates anomalies by randomly partitioning feature spaces. While highly accurate, evaluating an ensemble of 100 trees on a live stream adds substantial latency ($5-15\text{ms}$), which risks violating our strict $10\text{ms}$ overall SLA.
    *   *Option C: Autoencoder Reconstruction Error.* Deep learning approach where high reconstruction loss indicates an anomaly. This is too heavy for a $<10\text{ms}$ SLA unless using heavily compressed, shallow, or quantized models.
    *   *Decision:* I will select the **Mahalanobis Distance** method because it is mathematically rigorous, simple to run, and has a tiny computational footprint during live serving.

3.  **Low-Latency Production Architecture:**
    *   **Offline Training Pipeline:** 
        Every night, a batch job analyzes historical transactions, filters out known fraudulent events, and calculates the robust mean vector $\mu$ and the robust inverse covariance matrix $\Sigma^{-1}$ using a minimum covariance determinant (MCD) estimator to prevent outliers from distorting the covariance structure.
    *   **Online Feature Store / Inference:**
        The computed parameters ($\mu$ and $\Sigma^{-1}$) are loaded into the memory of our high-throughput prediction service.
        When a new transaction arrives, the service computes the Mahalanobis distance inline:
        ```python
        # Mathematically optimal, low-level execution (O(d^2) complexity)
        diff = x - mu
        anomaly_score = np.dot(np.dot(diff, inv_cov), diff.T)
        ```
        If the `anomaly_score` exceeds a threshold corresponding to a Chi-Square distribution critical value (e.g., $\chi^2_{df=2, \alpha=0.001}$), the transaction is flagged as a multivariate outlier and routed to an extra authentication flow.

### 3.3 Quickfire Deep-Dive Questions

#### Q: "Why don't you use KNN Imputation for real-time web-scale inference?"
*   **A:** KNN Imputation requires calculating the distance from the un-imputed target instance to all training instances in the entire dataset:
    $$\mathcal{O}(N \times d)$$
    where $N$ is the number of rows (millions/billions) and $d$ is the dimensions. Even with spatial indexing trees (like KD-trees), this lookup scaling scales horribly in production and cannot meet sub-millisecond real-time requirements. It is also highly memory-intensive because the reference dataset must be kept in memory.

#### Q: "If your target label $y$ is highly skewed, should you apply Box-Cox to it?"
*   **A:** Yes, but with caution. Transforming $y$ to a normal distribution helps linear regression satisfy homoscedasticity assumptions and stabilizes loss updates during training. 
    However, when evaluating performance or serving predictions, you must apply the **inverse transform** to return the predictions to the original physical scale:
    $$\hat{y}_{original} = (\lambda \hat{y}_{transformed} + 1)^{1/\lambda}$$
    Failing to do this means your evaluation metrics (like RMSE) and outputted predictions will be calculated on an arbitrary, compressed scale.

#### Q: "What is the breakdown point of an outlier detection method, and why do we care?"
*   **A:** The breakdown point is the proportion of incorrect observations an estimator can handle before it begins providing arbitrarily large or incorrect results. 
    For example, the mean has a breakdown point of $0$ (a single massive outlier can pull the mean to infinity). 
    The median has a breakdown point of $0.5$ ($50\%$ of the data must be corrupted to ruin it). 
    In production pipelines with high noise or potential data corruption, we must use estimators with high breakdown points (like MCD for covariance estimation or IQR for scaling) to ensure the preprocessing itself does not become a vector for pipeline instability.
